Time Is a Snake Biting Its Own Tail

12 March 2026
reading

Márquez lays out a peculiar notion of time in the rise and fall of the Buendía family. It is not linear, not simply cyclical, but something closer to a Möbius strip — you think you have arrived somewhere new, then look down and find the footprints are your own.

The first José Arcadio founded Macondo. He carried an unquenchable curiosity, trying to understand the world through magnets and alchemy. That impulse echoes through his descendants. Each generation believes itself original; none realises it is repeating.

All moments exist simultaneously.

The famous opening sentence stacks three temporal layers in a single line. That is not mere cleverness — it is the novel’s philosophy in miniature.

A Genealogy of Solitude

The solitude in the title is not a mood. It is a condition of existence. Every Buendía is sealed inside a private world. Between them there is blood, love, hatred — but never true understanding.

When I closed the book, the wind happened to pick up outside. A leaf was lifted, turned one circle in the air, and fell back to the same spot.
